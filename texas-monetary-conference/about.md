## About the Conference

---

<br/>

The Texas Monetary Conference is an annual conference bringing together leading researchers in monetary economics, financial theory, and macroeconomics. The conference is hosted by the Department of Economics at Rice University in Houston, Texas.

**Organizers:** Zach Bethune, Shihan Shen, and David Zhang

## The Texas Monetary Conference Story, 1993–2015

<i>Mark A. Wynne, Federal Reserve Bank of Dallas</i>

<br/>

The idea for the Texas Monetary Conference grew out of a conversation between Evan Koenig and Scott Freeman in the cafeteria of the Dallas Fed sometime in 1992 or early 1993. The first conference was hosted by UT Austin in 1993, but jointly organized with the Dallas Fed, with UT (Scott) inviting 2/3 of the speakers and the Dallas Fed (Evan) inviting the other 1/3, with conference costs being allocated the same way. The original plan was that hosting responsibilities would flip to Dallas the following year, along with cost allocations, and that the conference would alternate between UT and the Dallas Fed every other year. However, after the success of the first conference the roster of hosting institutions was opened up to include Texas A&M, Rice University and SMU. The University of Houston joined later. The idea behind the conference was to grow the macroeconomics / monetary economics research community in Texas by bringing together researchers working in monetary economics broadly defined at the major research institutions to learn more about cutting edge research in the field. The model for the conference was to have presentations from prominent academics from outside Texas, with all discussants drawn from Texas universities. The idea was that the conference would take place in a different location each year, typically in the spring, with the host institution bearing the bulk of the costs and the other institutions making contributions towards overall costs. Over the years, the convention has evolved that the host institution organizes the program as well.

Scott was a monetary economist who earned his PhD in economics at the University of Minnesota in 1983, where he studied under Neil Wallace, and joined the faculty of University of Texas – Austin in 1991. He began his academic career at Boston College, where he was awarded tenure, before moving to the University of California at Santa Barbara in 1988, and then to the University of Texas at Austin in 1991, where he spent the remainder of his career. Over the course of his academic career, he published over twenty peer-reviewed articles on various aspects of monetary economics in journals such as the *Journal of Political Economy*, the *American Economic Review*, the *Journal of Monetary Economics* and others of comparable prestige. His book with Bruce Champ *Modeling Monetary Economies* was published by John Wiley in 1994; a second edition was published by Cambridge University Press in 2001. Joe Haslag — a former colleague of Scott's at the Dallas Fed (where Scott was a research associate in the early 1990s) and a sometime co-author of his — joined as a co-author on the third (2011), fourth (2016) and fifth (2022) editions. Scott passed away from complications of ALS in 2004.

At the Dallas Fed the main liaison for many years was Evan Koenig. Evan joined the Bank in 1988 after an initial position as an assistant professor at the University of Washington. He headed up the macroeconomics group, eventually rising to become Senior Vice President and Principal Policy adviser to Dallas Fed presidents Richard Fisher and Rob Kaplan. Evan was an early champion of the need to account for the fact that early vintage data releases were subject to revisions that were sometimes quite significant and that forecasting models needed to take account of that fact. His most cited paper (according to Google Scholar) is his 2003 paper in the *Review of Economics and Statistics* on "The Use and Abuse of Real-Time Data in Economic Forecasting" (with Sheila Dolmas and Jeremy Piger). He was also a big champion of nominal GDP targeting as a strategy for monetary policy.

Another champion of the conference in its early years was Leonardo "Pepe" Auernheimer (1936–2010) who taught at Texas A&M from 1973 until his death in 2010. Pepe received his PhD from the University of Chicago where he studied under Milton Friedman and was a member of the famous Money and Banking Workshop. He first came to fame with his paper on "The Honest Government's Guide to the Revenue from the Creation of Money" which was based on his dissertation and published in the May/June 1974 issue of the *Journal of Political Economy*.

The principal liaisons at Rice University in the early years were John Bryant and Peter Hartley. John was a monetary theorist who joined Rice University from the Federal Reserve Bank of Minneapolis in 1981 after earning his PhD at Carnegie Mellon University in 1975. His best-known work was his brilliant 1983 four-page paper in the *Quarterly Journal of Economics* "A Simple Rational Expectations Keynes-Type Model" which helped reconcile the Keynesian insight on the role for government in stabilizing economic activity with the belief in the early years of the rational expectations revolution that in an economy where agents had rational (rather than, say, adaptive) expectations the role of government was minimal. Peter's early research was in traditional monetary economics and macroeconomics (having obtained his PhD in 1980 from the University of Chicago under Robert E. Lucas, Jr.) but moved into more energy related research in the later 1990s.

And finally, at SMU the main liaison over the first couple of decades of the conference was Nathan Balke who is best known for his work with Tom Fomby on threshold cointegration (published in the *International Economic Review* in 1997) and his estimates of prewar GNP created with Robert J. Gordon and published in the *Journal of Political Economy* in 1989.

The basic model for the conference has been to invite seven to nine academics from outside Texas to come present their most recent work. The priority has always been to get the best papers or speakers, with little attention paid to ensuring that they were linked by a common theme (other than being about macroeconomics or monetary economics broadly defined). However, there were at least two exceptions to this, as shown by the conference programs in the appendix. In 2009 the University of Texas – Austin organized the conference with the title "20 Years of Search Theory in Money and Finance". This was by far the biggest ever installment of the conference, with a total of eleven speakers. It was jointly organized with the Finance Department at University of Texas and included several out of state discussants and session chairs, a departure from conference convention. The 2014 installment of the conference, hosted by the Federal Reserve Bank of Dallas and organized by Carlos Zarazaga, was organized around the theme of the fiscal theory of the price level (an abiding interest of Carlos'!) and featured paper presentations by three of the leading developers of the fiscal theory (John Cochrane, Eric Leeper and Christopher Sims). We might also note that the 2003 conference — the first to be hosted and organized by the University of Houston (with the assistance of the Dallas Fed's Houston branch) — had a strong international macroeconomics flavor, reflecting the interest of the conference organizer that year, David Pappell.

Over the years the conference has featured a number of speakers who subsequently went on to win Nobel Prizes in economics (Ed Prescott and Finn Kydland (joint in 2004), Christopher Sims (joint with Thomas Sargent in 2011), and Daron Acemoglu (joint with James Robinson and Simon Johnson in 2024)). The conference also hosted the current secretary of the Nobel Prize committee (Per Krusell). Two presenters subsequently became presidents of Federal Reserve Banks — Jim Bullard who spoke at the 2002 conference and was president of the Federal Reserve Bank of St. Louis from 2008 to 2023, and Narayana Kocherlakota who spoke at the 1999 and 2002 conferences and was president of the Federal Reserve Bank of Minneapolis from 2009 to 2015. Richard Clarida, who spoke at the first conference in the series hosted by the Federal Reserve Bank of Dallas in 1994 went on to become Vice Chairman of the Board of Governors of the Federal Reserve System from 2018 to 2022.
